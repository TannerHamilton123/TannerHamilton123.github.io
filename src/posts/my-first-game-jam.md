# My first game jam

As a moderate fan of video games and professional interest in software development, I find game development to be an interesting outlet in both abstract creativity and technical problem solving. I had made a gravity puzzle game for myself and friends, as well as a promotional endless run for a company. This time, however, I participated in a game jam, where individuals or teams of developers compete in creating a game on a specific theme within a specific amount of time.

The theme was “break the bank”, making a game about financial literacy for Cool Math Game’s education course. I had created a small financial simulator before in Godot, as well as a few other games, and I have a side interest in finance (I’ve read a 1 and a half Michael Lewis books, so I’m pretty much a finance bro).


## The Mindset of Artistic Endeavors

I was sure I would win, which I thought was something to be ashamed of. Too much pride is a flaw that can lead to trouble. However, it is also a prerequisite for any endeavor. Overconfidence is necessary when pursuing a goal. An unjustified certainty pushes you into untested waters and into areas that are more challenging than you were expecting, and exactly where you need to be. 

I felt very dedicated and near manic in my development. My confidence was through the roof when I submitted it, seeing other games and thinking with such hubris that I was sure to win.
It took about a month for Cool math games to judge them, and in that time my neurotic fixation settled down into dissatisfaction and self-loathing. I looked back on the games with fresher eyes and saw the dedication and creativity that so many others put into their games as well. Did I really think that I cared more about my game than these people did about their’s? I was so sure about my own and so dismissive of my competition. At this point I felt ashamed and sure that I wouldn’t win. 

## Code organization. 
It's pretty easy to come up with a solution. It's hard to make that solution work with your other solutions. It's even harder to keep track of all the solutions you’ve stitched together. I generally work in large groups of professionals for engineering problems, so indie development really makes me sit with the consequences of my own actions. There’s no one else to blame when I have to follow a variable thread through 4 different files.

The script to run the game could have sat in a single file. There is no pragmatic reason for separating it out into multiple files except for the sanity of your own mind. I did my best to stay disciplined in keeping mechanics in their own scripting, like those that handle time, events, financial transactions, etc. However, these are constantly interacting with each other, so the categories blur and I start to forget whether events_handling or price_handling is the one that actually changes the prices during an event!

## User Interface
An adage that a project manager told me about engineer drawings is that, “it's not good if it doesn't look good.” Meaning that even if our calculations of information are correct, if our plans are consistently designed, then the client will find an excuse not to like it. UI isn’t something I’ve cared much about, but games are all about conveying complex ideas in the simplest way possible. Also, having the UI reinforce the feeling of player action and autonomy heightens the experience. 



## Personal Project Management
It was just me (and my girlfriend who made all the art and playtested it the most!) and a single deadline of when to submit the game. My other games were just side-interests with no external motivators, so I would work on it when I wanted, and step away when I wasn’t in the mood. My work ethic of Boba Broker was more consistent, enduring through the cycles of excitement and frustration and misery and nihilism. I can get a little dramatic, so it was helpful to have a reason to keep working through it even when I thought it was the worst thing I had ever made. That’s a mindset that I think both engineering and art rely on heavily: an endurance through the phases of uncertainty, yet still applying your best effort at solving the problem laid out in front of you with integrity and vigour.

## Scope Creep
Just like in any engineering project, you get sparks of inspiration or insight during the development process, or someone recommends a feature to improve your product. Soon, these pile up until you are developing something far beyond what you initially intended.
I see this as a natural and healthy part of the technical and creative process. Ideas are thought up or shared, and we have the willingness to entertain them and imagine their benefits if incorporated. But when those ideas meet the reality of timeline, goals, and technical competency, they can potentially asphyxiate a creative endeavor.

Things can always be better. Given the time and attention, a project can be improved upon and made into something more. But time and attention are the most important resources we have, and are inherently finite. I think about 4000 Weeks: Time Management for Mortals by Oliver Burkeman when considering the allure of improvement and perfection. Acknowledging the imperfection of your work is a practice in accepting your limited time on this planet.
