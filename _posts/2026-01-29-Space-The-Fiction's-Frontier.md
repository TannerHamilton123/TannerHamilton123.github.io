# Space: The Fiction's Frontier


## Outer Wilds
### The Ballad of clumsy Astronaut

Outer Wild’s tested my understanding of perception, astronomic motions, and navigating a zero-gravity environment. “Quantum “ puzzles are the forefront of the game, with objects disappearing when you are not viewing them, similar to the way a particle changes when under observation. This dynamic was a new element on viedo games that I had rarely seen. Most of my other experiences were around purely euclidian physics and static objects. Portal, another famous puzzle game, did create new senses of space and dynamism through areas using portals. A similar mechanic is available in Outer Wild’s, but more of a mystery and puzzle of unlucking these portals even to begin with. 

I took on the game like a research project, and it helped to have an in-game research board that tracked discoveries and landmarks. I would read through documents I had skimmed over on my first pass in order to gleam a hint about how to use ancient alien technology, a black box that I was trying to teleport straight into. Each planet held interconnected information that required all pieces to properly put together. Not like grabbing keys and running to doors, but to be knowledgable of the environment and how to navigate it to the next clue. Sands rose to cover secret caves, only available once you found the secret passage to get there in time. Machines would open their power only when a certain astronomical alignment was in place. Time was a crucial factor, and of course a pivotal aspect of the game’s gameloop and story. I became fluent in the motions of my ship and my jetpack. This came with learning, after a few times not decelerating enough and crashing into the planet, or jumping so high I left the orbit of a planet with no way of returning, simply running the clock until the sun would explode and I would start again.

The story of the game blended so well with the puzzle and gameplay that it had. A delicate astronaut exploring the world in a timeloop. Even though every other aspect of the universe repeats itself, you are the quantum particle, the one variable that changes every cycle. 

## Three Body Problem
### Science in Crisis

Three Body Problem is a story about absolutism, authority, religion, and of course science. The opening story of the Cultural Revolution was painful and concerning, and created an empathetic backstory for the “villain.” The book emphasized the brutality of both culture, physics, and our relationship with science. It veered in directions I was not expecting, and I felt almost deflated when the mystery was revealed, but as I recollect I see that may have been the point. The unknown only really has power when it is left in darkness, but once truth shines on the sober and mundane reality, it loses the grandiosity. Which is funny, since the truth is as grandiose as it gets. 

One aspect that I found interesting, yet it did not delve too much into, is a faith in science. Science intends to answer all questions we have about the world, yet it is far from there. The mechanism for answering questions is to use empirical data to record the world and predict outcomes. But what happens when we cannot trust the empirical nature of our reality. When we repeat the exact same thing, and get a different result? The book poses a crisis of faith in science, when people are led to believe that there can be no progress because the basis of scientific thought is based on a falsity. 

Of course, the source of this crisis is explained and debunked using… science. There is a parallelism between this crisis and the fervor the Chinese Cultural Revolution. Schools of thought (especially science) were cast out because of their percieved political alignment. The delusions of the revolution were more comforting or favorable than scientific thought. Just as the main characters of the present storyline, the characters in the revolution were discouraged from science because it did not align with a political belief. 

The science of this book is a tour-de-force. A subplot of the story follows the progression of a virtual civilization using various methods to understanding massive problems, from soothsaying to observational models to high computation. Astronomical motion and interstellar communication are described with detail and accessibility. Some parts were a little bit winded (I didn’t need to know which part of the Panama Canal was the widest and least busy) but most of it was enjoyable to dive into. 

## Real Fiction
### Science in Books and Games
Science Fiction certainly isn’t new in literature, but I think we are seeing a golden age for video games. As open worlds and graphics become have grown, the opportunity to explore a solar system, or even a galaxy, in your very own ship, has become a possibility in a virtual environment. More and more games offer this type of experience, like No Man’s Land, Star Citizen, and Eve Online. They’ve certainly grabbed the attention of my demographic (young nerdy guys chronically online). 

The reason with science fiction in books and video games feels so much more appealing to me than movies is the requirement for interaction. Reading a story requires the attention and creativity to imagine the not-yet-possible and create a world inside your head with new rules for how the fiction works. 

Video games come prebuilt with the creativity and rules, but the experience of interacting with a warphole or navigating a slingshot orbit around a sun fosters an experimental and curious mindset, to see how the mechanics of this world work, and how to use it for your means. It also creates an extremely accessible way to learn about complex problems in physics- like moving your character around a tight spot in zero-g - or experiencing scientific phenomena, like the death of a star.

The small game I made called Space Lander was a joy to make, but its also an interesting learning tool and seeing how people react to a (very modest) space simulation. The act of landing a space ship is hard, even when its two-dimensional and you’ve only got 4 button.

I don’t see fiction as meerly an escape from reality, but as a reflection and exploration of it. Experiential data is always first and foremost, but I currently can’t afford my own spaceship, nor have I yet to contact an alien civilization. Fiction can be whatever we want, but I think its best when it explores reality.
